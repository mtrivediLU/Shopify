§ 01

How I think about this work.

Integration is mostly about boundaries. Most of the backend work I've shipped has lived in the seam between a merchant's source-of-truth — an ERP, a fulfillment system, a Salesforce org, a commerce platform — and whatever the user-facing system is. The enjoyable part is the architecture. The hard part is what happens after launch: retries, partial failures, the question from a delivery manager who can't see why an order didn't sync. I try to think about that side of the work first, not last.

Production is where the craft shows. Anyone can ship something on a green field. The signal I've trained myself to look for is what the same system looks like six months in, when the original author has moved on and something fails at four in the morning. I aim for the second kind of system — boring, observable, recoverable — even when nobody is asking for it yet.

AI is leverage, not autopilot. I use Cursor, Claude, and the OpenAI and Gemini APIs daily — for ramping up on unfamiliar codebases, for first drafts, for exploring designs I wouldn't have reached on my own. The accountability stays with me. If a line of code lands in production under my name, I've read it, tested it, and I can explain why it's there.

§ 02

Where my background maps onto the role.

Four honest mappings, not seven. Where it's a clean fit, I say so. Where it isn't yet, I name it in section 06.

  1. The role asks for

    Custom integrations between commerce and merchant systems.

    Seven years of this in shape, if not in stack. At TCS, I led backend implementation on thirteen B2B platforms on SAP Commerce Cloud, all of them with ERP, fulfillment, and payment-adjacent connections. At Flosonics I built dbt-managed PostgreSQL services pulling from Salesforce and product systems. The technology was different. The work — clean contracts, predictable failure modes, replayable pipelines — is the same.

  2. The role asks for

    Primary technical contact on paid engagements.

    This has been most of my last seven years. At TCS the engagements were larger and longer; at LoopX and Minax they were shorter and rougher around the edges. In all of them I owned the technical thread end-to-end — discovery, design, code, validation, handover — including the conversations where I had to surface a risk early to a stakeholder who didn't want to hear it.

  3. The role asks for

    Backend, APIs, webhooks, reliable data flow.

    Java with Spring MVC and Hibernate is where I've spent the most production hours. Node.js and Python are where most of my recent work lives. The principles I lean on — idempotency keys, signed webhooks, bounded retries, dead-letter handling, rate-limit awareness — port directly to Rails and Sidekiq, even though those tools are newer to me. See section 03 for what that looks like in code.

  4. The role asks for

    AI tools as an accelerator, not a crutch.

    I work in Cursor on most days. I keep Claude open as a second pair of eyes. At Flosonics I shipped internal AI assistants in Python that reduced recurring reporting effort roughly forty percent — which mattered less than the discipline that came with it: writing prompts as if they were specs, treating model output as a draft, never as a deliverable. I expect to use these tools the same way at Shopify.

§ 03

A working sketch.

To keep this from being claims-without-code: a small Rails-style sketch of how I'd handle the first inch of a Shopify webhook integration. Not a real codebase — just enough to show the thinking that I'd want a senior reviewer to grade. I've been working through Rails 7 in preparation for the pair-programming round; this is the kind of thing I'd reach for first.

app/controllers/shopify_webhooks_controller.rb 
# Receives Shopify webhooks. The only goals here are:
# 1) verify the request is actually from Shopify,
# 2) acknowledge it fast,
# 3) make sure we never process the same event twice.
# Real work happens later, in a background job.

class ShopifyWebhooksController < ApplicationController
  skip_before_action :verify_authenticity_token

  def receive
    raw_body = request.body.read

    # Reject anything we can't authenticate. Constant-time compare.
    unless valid_hmac?(raw_body, request.headers["X-Shopify-Hmac-Sha256"])
      return head(:unauthorized)
    end

    event_id = request.headers["X-Shopify-Webhook-Id"]

    # Idempotency: same delivery, same outcome. Shopify retries; we shrug.
    if WebhookEvent.exists?(shopify_event_id: event_id)
      return head(:ok)
    end

    WebhookEvent.create!(
      shopify_event_id: event_id,
      topic:            request.headers["X-Shopify-Topic"],
      shop_domain:      request.headers["X-Shopify-Shop-Domain"],
      payload:          raw_body
    )

    # Hand off and acknowledge fast — keep this controller boring.
    ProcessWebhookJob.perform_later(event_id)
    head :ok
  end

  private

  def valid_hmac?(body, header)
    return false if header.blank?

    digest = OpenSSL::HMAC.digest("sha256", ENV.fetch("SHOPIFY_API_SECRET"), body)
    expected = Base64.strict_encode64(digest)

    ActiveSupport::SecurityUtils.secure_compare(expected, header)
  end
end

What I'd add next, in order. A ProcessWebhookJob as a Sidekiq worker that does the real work with exponential backoff and a poison-message limit. A dead_letter_events table for anything past that limit, with a small admin view so a delivery manager can replay manually instead of pinging engineering. Structured logs keyed on shop_domain and shopify_event_id so a support question takes thirty seconds, not thirty minutes. Tests on the controller for the unauthorized, duplicate, and happy paths — they're cheap and they catch the things that actually break in production.

The pattern isn't novel. The discipline of treating the webhook controller as a thin authenticated front door, and pushing everything that can fail into a place where failure is recoverable, is what I want to bring on day one.

§ 04

Trophies and bruises.

Shopify's candidate guide says the Life Story conversation is interested in the trophies and the bruises. That framing has stuck with me, so I'll start there.

Trophies.

Thirteen B2B platforms, one playbook.

The TCS years were a slow accumulation of pattern-recognition. By the time I was leading a team of four, I could look at a new enterprise commerce engagement and know within a week what was going to bite us six months later. That sense — "this contract is too loose, that handoff is going to fail under load" — is the part I'd most like to bring forward.

Internal AI assistants that people actually used.

The win at Flosonics wasn't the percentage of effort saved. It was that the operations team started writing their own prompts and asking better questions of their own data. The tool faded into the background and the workflow changed. That's the version of "AI as leverage" I want to keep building.

The mining-field deployment that survived.

LoopX was unglamorous and useful. Offline-capable workflows for people who don't think of themselves as software users, signed off in UAT by operators on a noisy floor, with a handover document that the customer team still owns. Most of the credit goes to taking the requirements meeting seriously.

Bruises.

Observability is not a phase-two concern.

Earlier in my career I shipped an integration that worked beautifully in development and was effectively a black box once it hit production. The first incident took two days to diagnose. Now I scaffold structured logging, basic metrics, and an honest README in the first commit, not the last. It's slower up front and a lot faster every other week after that.

Verbal contracts age badly.

I have shipped at least one integration where the upstream field shape was agreed in a meeting and never written down. The upstream team renamed the field. Three downstream consumers found out simultaneously. Now there is always a written contract, a version number, and a deprecation note before anything ships — even when it feels like ceremony.

Incidents are feedback, not exceptions.

I used to treat production incidents as one-offs to be fixed and forgotten. The same class of bug usually surfaces three times before someone fixes the underlying habit. I now write a one-page note after anything that woke me up — what failed, what I did, what I'd change in the design — and circulate it before the memory fades.

§ 06

Honest gaps, and the first ninety days.

If I'm being graded on "fit, right now," there are three places I'd flag before someone else does.

Ruby on Rails in production.

I have shipped Java Spring backends for years. The mental model — request lifecycle, ORM patterns, background jobs, middleware — ports directly. The Rails syntax and conventions are not yet muscle memory. I'd expect the first thirty days to be a real ramp, not a cosmetic one.

Production Shopify app development.

I haven't shipped a Shopify app yet. I've worked with Salesforce, SAP Commerce, and Microsoft APIs at length, so the shape — auth, webhooks, rate limits, GraphQL — is familiar. The Shopify-specific patterns (Polaris, app extensions, App Bridge, Functions) are net-new and I'd expect to spend deliberate time there.

Working alongside Plus merchants directly.

I've been the primary technical contact on enterprise engagements, but the merchants I've worked with weren't on Shopify Plus. The communication patterns translate. The product context doesn't, yet.

Days 1 – 30

Charge the trust battery from zero.

Read the codebases I'm assigned to. Sit on at least two active engagements as a quiet observer. Ship one small, well-scoped PR every week — not for visibility, for calibration. By day thirty, I want a senior on the team to feel comfortable handing me a bug that touches real merchant code.

Days 31 – 60

Take a defined slice end-to-end.

Pick up a scoped piece of an active integration — ideally one that has a clear merchant outcome and a manageable surface area. Discovery through implementation, with a senior reviewer in the loop. The goal is one shipped artifact I can point to in a 1:1 and say "this is the level I'm operating at."

Days 61 – 90

Own a merchant-facing workstream.

By day ninety, the goal is to be the engineer a Delivery Manager can hand work to and trust it lands — including the awkward calls. Not the most senior person on the team. Just useful, predictable, and visibly improving.

§ 07

A short closing.

I've watched Shopify from the outside for a long time. Two things stand out, both of which I read about long before I applied.

The first is that engineering craft, merchant outcomes, and business success are talked about in the same sentence — which is rarer than it sounds. The second is the ~Mastery framing of growth without forcing crafters onto a management track. That's the model I wish more companies used, and it's why this posting is the one I sat down to write a real follow-up for instead of just clicking apply.

I'd be glad to keep going.
